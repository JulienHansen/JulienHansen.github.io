## A small proof

**Claim.** For all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $\sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$.

*Proof.* By induction on $n$.

**Base case.** For $n = 1$, the left side is $1$ and the right side is
$\frac{1 \cdot 2}{2} = 1$. ✓

**Inductive step.** Assume the identity holds for some $n \geq 1$. Then

$$
\sum_{k=1}^{n+1} k = \left(\sum_{k=1}^{n} k\right) + (n+1)
= \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + (n+1)
= \frac{(n+1)(n+2)}{2},
$$

which is the formula with $n+1$ in place of $n$. $\blacksquare$
